5.1 Introduction to Counting Process

In the study of theory of probability, we do not only study individual objects, we also would index various things into categories and discuss their relationships with one another. That is known as stochastic process, which is a mathematical object usually deﬁned as a family of random variables.

We will be looking at two kinds of stochastic processes in this notebook. One is counting process that includes Poisson process and Renewal Theory. This will be elaborated in this Chapter. The other kind of stochastic process is Markov chain which will be studied in the next Chapter.

Before learning about speciﬁc kinds of counting process, let us have a brief understanding of what a counting process is.

A counting process is a stochastic process {N(t),t ≥ 0} with values that are nonnegative, integer and non-decreasing, or equivalently:

1. N(t) ≥ 0.

2. N(t) is an integer.

3. If s ≤ t, then N(s) ≤ N(t).

Also, we should note that t ∈ [0,∞), which implies ‘time’ is continuous here.

If Nt describes an arrival process, then Nt = k means that there have been k arrivals in the time interval [0,t]. In fact, we can describe the process by the sequence of arrival times, which we might call “points” of the process. Let Tk = inf{t ≥ 0 : Nt ≥ k} for k ≥ 0. Then T0 = 0 and Tk is the “k-th arrival time” for k ≥ 1. We also deﬁne Y k = Tk −Tk−1 for k ≥ 1. Here the Y k is the “inter-arrival time” between arrivals k − 1 and k.

In addition, for s < t, we write N(s,t] for Nt − Ns, which we can think of as the number of points of the process which occur in the time-interval (s,t]. This is also called the “increment” of the process N on the interval (s,t].

5.2 Homogeneous Poisson Process

The name ‘Poisson’ is used in the name of the process for a reason. The other thing that is named after the same person is the Poisson distribution, which is a discrete distribution modelling the occurrence of an unlikely event. The readers who have forgotten about it can refer to Chapter 3.3. The reason why this type of stochastic process is named after Poisson is because, although Poisson never actually worked on this at all during his life time, it follows the same Poisson distribution to some extent. We will see that connection more closely as we proceed with this topic.

A stream of events occurring one by one in continuous time, in an uncoordinated way can be modelled by a Poisson process. For example, it can very accurately model the process of times of detections by a Geiger counter near a radioactive source, or it can be a good model for the process of times of arrivals of calls at a call centre.

5.3 Superposition and Thinning

A Poisson process can be superposed, and it can also be thinned.

For superposition, if we have two independent Poisson processes Lt and Mt with rate λ and μ respectively, and let Nt = Lt + Mt, we get a Poisson process Nt with rate λ + μ. This can be shown by simple manipulations of the deﬁnitions, which will be omitted.

For thinning, if we have a Poisson process Nt with rate λ. We mark each point of the process with probability p independently. We then let the counting process of the marked points be Mt, it is then a Poisson process of rate pλ. Proofs will be skipped.

Their is a stronger statement of thinning. With the same setting, we can single out the unmarked points and let the counting process of those points be L, that is again a counting process of rate (1 −p)λ and it is independent of M. This is quite logical if we think about the superposition property of N = M + L.

An application of the extended version of thinning can be used to study the queueing system M/G/1.

5.4 Nonhomogeneous Poisson Process

So far, we have only discussed the case of constant rate, also known as the homogeneous case. The rate of the Poisson process can in fact be changing. In that case, the Poisson process will be nonhomogeneous, since they are not of the same ‘kind’ any more.

To better illustrate the case of nonhomogeneous Poisson process, we ﬁrst introduce a third way to deﬁne a homogeneous Poisson process.

Before going into the deﬁnition, we will need to deﬁne something else. A function f is said to be o(h) if lim(h → 0) = 0.

Now, for a counting process {N(t),t ≥ 0}, it is said to be a Poisson process with rate λ if

1. N(0) = 0.

2. The process has stationary and independent increments.

3. P{N(h) = 1} = λh + o(h).

4. P{N(h) ≥ 2} = o(h).

Here, stationary increment means the distribution of the number of events that occur in any interval of time depends only on the length of the time interval. Additionally, just like the two ways before, this deﬁnition is equivalent to the other.

Knowing those, we are ready to give the deﬁnition of nonhomogeneous Poisson process.

For a counting process {N(t),t ≥ 0}, it is said to be a nonhomogeneous or nonstationary Poisson process with intensity function λ(t) if

1. N(0) = 0.

2. The process has independent increments.

3. P{N(t + h) − N(t) = 1} = λ(t)h + o(h).

4. P{N(t + h) − N(t) ≥ 2} = o(h).
